LOCAL STUDENT DISCOVERS 3AM IS NOT OPTIMAL STUDY TIME

By Our Sleep-Deprived Correspondent

In a groundbreaking discovery that shocked exactly no one, local student Adriza Aryal confirmed yesterday that 3:00 AM is, in fact, not the best time for productive studying. The revelation came after the student attempted to solve calculus problems while battling what experts describe as "severe eyelid heaviness."

"I swear I understood integration at 2:45 AM, but by 3:15 it looked like hieroglyphics," the student reported.

Eyewitnesses report seeing the student alternating between frantic note-taking and staring blankly at the same physics equation for 27 minutes straight. "At one point they were annotating their notebook with what appeared to be drool," said roommate (actually her mom).

Education experts warn that this phenomenon, known as "Delusional Nocturnal Productivity Syndrome," affects thousands of Nepali students during exam season. Symptoms include:

  • Believing you're making progress when actually writing nonsense
  • Sudden urges to completely reorganize your study materials at 4 AM
  • Developing personal theories about how sleep is a capitalist conspiracy

The student in question reportedly powered through with a dangerous cocktail of instant coffee and determination, only to wake up with their face stuck to their chemistry textbook. "I think I dreamed the solutions," they mumbled before passing out for 14 hours straight.

KATHMANDU, TUESDAY —

BREAKING: MOM ASKS 'HAVE YOU EATEN?' FOR 17TH CONSECUTIVE DAY

By Our Well-Fed Correspondent

In what scientists are calling "the most predictable event in Nepali households," local mother [YOUR MOM'S NAME] today asked her child if they had eaten for the seventeenth consecutive day, despite the child having just finished a full meal in her presence.

"I literally just ate dal bhat in front of her," said Adriza Aryal, the subject of the inquiry. "She watched me have seconds. She served me the food. And yet, 27 minutes later: 'Khana khais?'"

Cultural anthropologists suggest this behavior stems from an ancient Nepali parenting algorithm where:

  1. If child is within 3 meters → Ask if they've eaten
  2. If child is eating → Ask if they want more
  3. If child is full → Ask when they'll eat next

"It's basically our version of 'I love you,'" explained Dr. Delusional of Tribhuvan University. "The Nepali maternal brain cannot compute that their offspring might be adequately nourished."

At press time, the student was seen sneaking snacks while claiming not to be hungry, in what experts describe as a classic case of "Reverse Nepali Teenage Logic."
